Is Matter Around Us Pure?

Explore pure substances, mixtures, solutions, colloids and how we separate them.

🔍 Pure Substances vs. Mixtures

All matter around us can be classified as either a pure substance or a mixture.

⚗️ Matter

📌 Pure Substance

A substance that has a uniform and definite composition. Cannot be separated into simpler substances by physical means.

Examples: Gold, Water (H₂O), Salt (NaCl), Sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁)

📌 Mixture

Contains two or more substances (elements or compounds) in any ratio. No chemical bonding between components. Properties of each component retained.

Examples: Air, Sea water, Soil, Alloys

⚛️ Elements and Compounds

🔵 Elements

A pure substance made of only one kind of atom. Cannot be broken down into simpler substances by any chemical method.

✅ Examples

Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Gold (Au), Iron (Fe), Carbon (C), Nitrogen (N)

118 elements are known. 94 occur naturally.

🟣 Compounds

A pure substance formed by chemical combination of two or more elements in a fixed ratio by mass. Has completely different properties from its constituent elements.

✅ Examples

Water (H₂O), Common salt (NaCl), Carbon dioxide (CO₂), Ammonia (NH₃)

🌍 Real Life: Element vs. Compound

Hydrogen and oxygen are both explosive gases — but combine them chemically and you get water, which puts out fires! This shows compounds have entirely different properties from their elements.

Uniform composition throughout. Components not visible separately.

= Solution
🔴🟡

Heterogeneous Mixture

Non-uniform composition. Components may be visible.

e.g. Soil, salad

🧴 Solutions (Homogeneous Mixtures)

📌 Solution

A homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. The substance present in larger amount is the solvent; the one dissolved is the solute.

🧂

Solute

Substance that gets dissolved. E.g., salt in saltwater.

💧

Solvent

Substance in which solute dissolves. E.g., water in saltwater.

🔵

Concentration

Amount of solute dissolved per unit volume of solution.

Concentration = (Mass of Solute / Volume of Solution) × 100
🌍 Real Life

🧃 Lemonade: Sugar + lemon juice (solutes) dissolved in water (solvent)
🩺 Saline drip in hospitals: NaCl solution (0.9%) — a controlled concentration solution
🌊 Sea water: Many salts dissolved in water
⛽ Petrol: A solution of several hydrocarbons

📌 Tyndall Effect

When a beam of light passes through a colloid, the path of light becomes visible due to scattering of light by colloidal particles. This is called the Tyndall Effect.

🌍 Tyndall Effect in Daily Life

🌄 Sunlight through forest trees: Dust/mist acts as colloid — beam of light visible
🚗 Car headlights in fog: Light beam visible due to scattering by fog droplets
🥛 Milk looks white: Fat globules scatter light — milk is a colloid!
🍨 Ice cream: A colloid — tiny ice crystals dispersed in cream

Activity

Observe Tyndall Effect at Home

  1. Make a solution of milk in water (few drops in a glass).
  2. Take it to a dark room.
  3. Shine a flashlight (torch) through the side of the glass.
  4. The beam of light becomes visible — this is the Tyndall Effect!

💡 This proves milk is a colloid, not a true solution!

🔬 Separation Techniques

Mixtures can be separated into their components using physical methods. The technique chosen depends on the nature of the mixture.

🧲 Magnetic Separation

A magnet is used to separate magnetic materials from non-magnetic ones.

📦 Use: Separating iron filings from sulfur powder. Used in recycling to separate iron/steel from waste.

🫧 Evaporation

A solution is heated so the solvent evaporates, leaving the solute behind.

📦 Use: Getting salt from sea water. Used in salt pans along coastlines — sun evaporates water, salt remains!

🌀 Centrifugation

Spinning a mixture at high speed causes denser particles to settle at the bottom.

📦 Use: Separating cream from milk, blood cells from plasma in hospitals, washing machine spin cycle removes water.

🔽 Decantation & Filtration

Decantation: Pouring off the liquid while leaving sediment behind.
Filtration: Passing mixture through a filter paper — residue stays, filtrate passes.

📦 Use: Filtering tea leaves, water treatment plants filter impurities, coffee filters.

🌡️ Distillation

Liquid mixture is heated — the component with lower boiling point vaporises first, then cools and collects separately.

📦 Use: Separating acetone from water, purifying water, making perfumes and alcoholic spirits.

📺 Chromatography

Separation based on different speeds of components travelling over a stationary phase (paper/silica) with a mobile phase (solvent).

📦 Use: Separating dyes in ink, testing purity of drugs/food colours, forensic analysis.

Activity

Paper Chromatography at Home

  1. Draw a thick line with a black sketch pen on filter paper, 2 cm from bottom.
  2. Dip the bottom edge in water in a glass (ink line should NOT touch water).
  3. Wait 10–15 minutes as water rises up the paper.
  4. Watch different colour components separate as water moves up!

❄️ Crystallisation

Separating a pure solid from an impure solution by allowing it to cool slowly — pure crystals form.

📦 Use: Purifying salt, sugar and copper sulphate. Alum crystals in lab. Refining sugar in factories.

🧠 Quick Quiz — Chapter 2

1. Which of the following is a pure substance?
✅ NaCl is a compound — a pure substance with a fixed composition. Air, sea water, and soil are all mixtures.
2. The Tyndall effect is shown by:
✅ Colloids show the Tyndall effect because their particles (1–100 nm) scatter light. True solutions have particles too small to scatter light.
3. Which technique is used to separate cream from milk in dairies?
✅ Centrifugation spins milk at high speed — denser components (water) move outward while lighter cream collects near the centre.
4. What is the dispersed phase in milk?
✅ In milk, fat globules are dispersed in water — making it a liquid-in-liquid colloid (emulsion). That's why it looks white and shows the Tyndall Effect!
