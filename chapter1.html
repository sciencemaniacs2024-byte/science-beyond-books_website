Matter in Our Surroundings

Understand what matter is, its three states, and how it changes from one form to another.

🔍 What is Matter?

Anything that has mass and occupies space is called matter. Look around — your desk, water in a glass, the air you breathe — all are matter!

📌 Definition

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space (volume). It is made up of tiny particles called atoms and molecules.

✅ Examples

Book, water, oxygen gas, iron nail, salt — all are matter.
Light, sound, heat — NOT matter (they have no mass or volume).

🌍 Real Life

When you hold a balloon filled with air, you can feel it has weight — that's the mass of air (matter) inside it!

⚡ Particle Nature of Matter

Matter is made of tiny particles that are too small to see with the naked eye. These particles have special characteristics:

🔗

Intermolecular Spaces

Particles have spaces between them. Solids have least, gases have most.

🔄

Constant Motion

Particles are always moving — vibrating in solids, freely in gases.

🧲

Forces of Attraction

Particles attract each other. Strongest in solids, weakest in gases.

Activity

Dissolving Sugar in Water

See particles in action!

  1. Take a glass of water and add 2 spoons of sugar.
  2. Stir gently until sugar dissolves completely.
  3. Taste it — it's sweet throughout the glass!

💡 Why? Sugar particles moved into the spaces between water particles — proving particles have spaces between them!

🧊 Three States of Matter

Matter exists in three main states depending on the arrangement and movement of its particles:

Solid

Fixed shape & volume

Liquid

Fixed volume, no fixed shape

Gas

No fixed shape or volume

Property 🧊 Solid 💧 Liquid 💨 Gas
Shape Definite Indefinite Indefinite
Volume Definite Definite Indefinite
Compressibility Negligible Low High
Fluidity None Can flow Flows easily
Particle spacing Very close Close Far apart
Particle movement Vibrate only Slide past each other Move freely
Intermolecular force Very strong Moderate Negligible
🌍 Real Life Examples

Solid: Ice, iron nail, wood
Liquid: Water, milk, mercury
Gas: Air, steam, LPG (cooking gas)

🔥 Change of State

Matter changes from one state to another when heat is added or removed. These changes are caused by change in temperature and pressure.

State Change Diagram

Changes of state — add heat (blue) or remove heat (green)

🧊→💧

Melting

Solid → Liquid. Temperature at which it happens = Melting Point. (Ice melts at 0°C)

💧→💨

Vaporisation

Liquid → Gas. At boiling point (100°C for water at 1 atm). Rapid process.

💨→💧

Condensation

Gas → Liquid. Steam touching a cold surface turns to water droplets.

💧→🧊

Freezing

Liquid → Solid. Water freezes at 0°C to become ice.

🧊→💨

Sublimation

Solid → Gas directly. Camphor, dry ice, naphthalene balls sublime.

📌 Latent Heat

The heat energy absorbed/released during a change of state without any change in temperature is called Latent Heat. This is why ice at 0°C feels colder than water at 0°C — ice needs to absorb latent heat to melt!

💧 Evaporation

Evaporation is the process by which a liquid converts into vapour below its boiling point, at the surface of the liquid.

📌 Key Concept

Only the faster-moving particles at the surface escape into the air. This is why evaporation is a surface phenomenon and causes cooling.

Factors Affecting Evaporation:

🌡️

Temperature ↑

Higher temperature → more particles have energy → faster evaporation

💨

Wind Speed ↑

Wind carries away vapour → air above becomes unsaturated → faster evaporation

💦

Humidity ↓

Low humidity = less water vapour already in air → faster evaporation

📐

Surface Area ↑

Larger surface → more particles exposed → faster evaporation

🌍 Real Life

🥵 Sweating cools us: Sweat evaporates from skin, taking away heat — we feel cool.
👕 Wet clothes dry: Water evaporates from fabric surface.
🏺 Earthen pots (matka): Water seeps through pores, evaporates, and cools the water inside.
🌊 Sea breeze: Evaporation from sea creates moisture in coastal air.

Activity

Feel Evaporation Cooling

  1. Put a few drops of acetone (nail polish remover) on your palm.
  2. Keep your palm still for a few seconds.
  3. Notice the cooling sensation as it evaporates!

💡 The acetone evaporates quickly, absorbing heat from your skin — so you feel cold!

🌫️ Diffusion

Diffusion is the spontaneous mixing of particles of two substances due to their random motion.

📌 Definition

The process by which particles of two different substances intermix on their own due to kinetic energy is called diffusion.

⭐ Key Points
  • Diffusion is fastest in gases, slower in liquids, slowest in solids
  • Rate of diffusion increases with temperature
  • Diffusion occurs from high concentration → low concentration
  • No external energy is needed
🌍 Real Life

🌹 Fragrance of flowers/perfume spreading across a room — diffusion of gases
🍵 Tea colour spreading in hot water without stirring — diffusion in liquids
🪴 Water absorption by roots — diffusion in semi-solids
🏭 Smoke rising from chimneys diffuses into the atmosphere

Activity

Diffusion in Liquids

  1. Fill a glass with still water.
  2. Carefully drop one crystal of potassium permanganate (purple) at the bottom.
  3. Do NOT stir. Observe over 10–15 minutes.
  4. The purple colour slowly spreads upward — diffusion!

🧠 Quick Quiz — Chapter 1

1. Which state of matter has a definite shape and definite volume?
✅ Correct! Solids have both definite shape and volume because particles are tightly packed with strong intermolecular forces.
2. Why does evaporation cause cooling?
✅ Evaporating particles take energy (heat) from the surroundings — so the surface left behind feels cool. This is called evaporative cooling.
3. Camphor disappears without forming a liquid. This process is called:
✅ Sublimation is the direct change from solid to gas without passing through the liquid state. Camphor, naphthalene balls and dry ice are classic examples.
4. In which medium is diffusion fastest?
✅ Gases have maximum intermolecular spaces and fastest-moving particles, so diffusion is most rapid in gases.
