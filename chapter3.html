Atoms and Molecules

From Dalton's theory to the mole concept — understand the building blocks of all matter.

🔭 Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)

John Dalton proposed that all matter is made of indivisible particles called atoms. His theory formed the foundation of modern chemistry.

⭐ Postulates of Dalton's Atomic Theory
  • All matter is made of very small particles called atoms
  • Atoms cannot be created, divided or destroyed (during chemical reactions)
  • All atoms of the same element are identical in mass and properties
  • Atoms of different elements have different masses and properties
  • Atoms combine in small whole-number ratios to form compounds
  • The relative number and kinds of atoms are constant in a given compound
🌍 Why This Matters

Dalton's theory explained why water is always H₂O — never H₃O or HO. Atoms always combine in fixed ratios, which is why every substance has a definite formula!

⚠️ Modern Modifications

We now know atoms CAN be divided (into protons, neutrons, electrons). Atoms of the same element can have different masses (isotopes). But Dalton's core ideas remain valid for explaining chemical reactions.

⚖️ Laws of Chemical Combination

Law 1: Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1789)

Mass can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. Total mass of reactants = Total mass of products.

🧪 Example: If 20g hydrogen + 160g oxygen react → exactly 180g water forms. Nothing is lost!

Law 2: Law of Constant Proportions (Proust, 1799)

In a chemical compound, elements are always present in fixed ratio by mass, regardless of source or method of preparation.

🧪 Example: Water always contains H and O in ratio 1:8 by mass. Whether from tap, rain or ocean — always the same!
Think About It

Law of Conservation — A Burning Candle

When a candle burns, it seems to disappear — but where does the mass go?

  1. Weigh a candle and place it under a sealed glass jar on a balance.
  2. Light it and let it burn for 5 minutes (sealed, so gases stay inside).
  3. Weigh again — the total mass stays the same!

💡 The wax converted to CO₂ and H₂O gases — mass didn't disappear, it changed form!

⚪ What is an Atom?

📌 Atom

The smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction. Atoms are extremely tiny — about 10⁻¹⁰ metres in size.

Each element has its own chemical symbol — a shorthand of 1 or 2 letters. Most symbols come from the element's English or Latin name.

✅ Symbol Origins

Na = Natrium (Sodium in Latin)
K = Kalium (Potassium in Latin)
Fe = Ferrum (Iron in Latin)
Au = Aurum (Gold in Latin)
O = Oxygen (English)

📌 Atomic Mass

The mass of one atom of an element compared to 1/12th the mass of a Carbon-12 atom. Measured in atomic mass units (u or amu).

1 u = 1.66 × 10⁻²⁴ grams — impossibly small!

📐 Calculating Molecular Mass

📌 Molecular Mass

Sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in one molecule of the substance.

✅ Worked Example — Water (H₂O)

H₂O contains 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom.
Atomic mass of H = 1 u, O = 16 u
Molecular mass of H₂O = 2(1) + 1(16) = 2 + 16 = 18 u

✅ Worked Example — Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄)

H₂SO₄ = 2H + 1S + 4O
= 2(1) + 1(32) + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 u

🔢 Valency and Writing Formulae

📌 Valency

The combining capacity of an atom — how many bonds it can form. Determined by the number of electrons in the outermost shell that participate in bonding.

Element/Group Symbol Valency
Hydrogen H 1
Oxygen O 2
Nitrogen N 3
Carbon C 4
Sodium Na⁺ 1
Calcium Ca²⁺ 2
Aluminium Al³⁺ 3
Chloride Cl⁻ 1
Sulphate SO₄²⁻ 2
Nitrate NO₃⁻ 1
⭐ Criss-Cross Method for Writing Formulae
  • Write the symbols of the two combining elements/ions
  • Write their valencies on top of each symbol
  • Criss-cross the valencies as subscripts
  • Reduce to the simplest whole-number ratio if needed
✅ Example: Aluminium Oxide

Al (valency 3) + O (valency 2)
Criss-cross: Al₂O₃
Formula: Al₂O₃ (aluminium oxide)

🔢 The Mole Concept

Atoms are incredibly tiny. We need a convenient unit to count them in bulk — that unit is the mole.

6.022 × 10²³
= 1 Mole = Avogadro's Number (Nₐ)

This is the number of atoms in exactly 12 grams of Carbon-12

📌 Mole

One mole of any substance contains 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, or ions). The mass of one mole is its molar mass in grams.

Molar Mass = Molecular Mass expressed in grams/mol
✅ Examples

1 mole of C = 12 g, contains 6.022×10²³ carbon atoms
1 mole of H₂O = 18 g, contains 6.022×10²³ water molecules
1 mole of NaCl = 58.5 g, contains 6.022×10²³ NaCl units

Number of moles = Given mass (g) ÷ Molar mass (g/mol)
Number of particles = Moles × 6.022 × 10²³
🌍 Why the Mole?

One atom of hydrogen weighs 1.67 × 10⁻²⁴ g — impossible to weigh! But 1 mole of hydrogen (6 × 10²³ atoms) weighs exactly 1 gram — measurable in a lab. The mole bridges the atomic world and our lab world!

🧠 Quick Quiz — Chapter 3

1. According to the Law of Conservation of Mass, what happens to mass during a chemical reaction?
✅ Lavoisier's Law: mass of reactants = mass of products. Atoms are only rearranged, never created or destroyed.
2. What is the molecular mass of CO₂? (C = 12, O = 16)
✅ CO₂ = 1C + 2O = 12 + 2(16) = 12 + 32 = 44 u. Carbon dioxide has a molecular mass of 44 u.
3. How many atoms are present in 1 mole of any element?
✅ Avogadro's number = 6.022 × 10²³. This is the number of particles in one mole of any substance.
4. What is the valency of Aluminium (Al)?
✅ Aluminium has 3 electrons in its outermost shell, so its valency is 3. Hence it forms Al³⁺ ions.
