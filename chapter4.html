Structure of the Atom

Discover electrons, protons, neutrons and the beautiful models scientists built to understand the atom.

⇛ Historical Atomic Models

Our understanding of the atom evolved over centuries through brilliant experiments. Here is the journey:

⇛ John Dalton (1808) ⇛ Solid Sphere Model

Proposed atoms as tiny, indivisible, solid spheres. All atoms of an element are identical.

Limitation: Could not explain electrical properties of matter.

⇛ J.J. Thomson (1897) ⇛ Plum Pudding Model

Discovered electrons. Proposed atom is a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded like plums in a pudding (raisins in bread).

Limitation: Rutherford's experiment proved this wrong the positive charge is concentrated at centre.

⇛ Ernest Rutherford (1911) ⇛ Nuclear Model

Gold foil experiment: shot alpha particles at gold foil. Most passed through, a few were deflected, some bounced back.

Conclusions: Atom is mostly empty space. A tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus exists at the centre. Electrons revolve around it.

Limitation: Could not explain why electrons don't lose energy and fall into nucleus.

⇛ Niels Bohr (1913) ⇛ Planetary Model

Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits (shells/energy levels) around the nucleus. Each orbit has a fixed energy. Electrons can jump between orbits by absorbing or emitting energy.

This is the model used in Class 9 NCERT. Shells are named K, L, M, N (or 1, 2, 3, 4).

Activity

Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

Simulate the experiment mentally:

  1. Imagine throwing tiny balls (alpha particles) at a very thin gold sheet.
  2. Most balls go straight through atom is mostly empty space.
  3. A few deflect they came close to something positive (nucleus).
  4. Very few bounce back they hit the tiny, dense nucleus head-on.

Rutherford said: "It was as if I fired artillery shells at tissue paper and they came back and hit me!"

Subatomic Particles

Atoms are made of three fundamental subatomic particles:

e-

Electron

Charge: -1
Mass: ~0 (negligible)
Location: Orbits (shells) around nucleus

Discovered by J.J. Thomson (1897)

p+

Proton

Charge: +1
Mass: 1 u
Location: Inside the nucleus

Discovered by Rutherford (1919)

n0

Neutron

Charge: 0 (neutral)
Mass: 1 u
Location: Inside the nucleus

Discovered by James Chadwick (1932)

* Real Life

Electricity: Flow of electrons through a wire. Electrons are the charge carriers!
Batteries: Store energy by moving electrons between terminals.
Static electricity: Rubbing a balloon transfers electrons it sticks to wall!
Nuclear energy: Energy from the nucleus splitting atoms (fission) in nuclear power plants.

Atomic Number and Mass Number

Atomic Number (Z)

The number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.
This uniquely identifies an element.

In a neutral atom: number of protons = number of electrons

Z = number of protons

Mass Number (A)

Total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.

A = Z + N
where N = number of neutrons

Therefore: N = A - Z

Neutrons = Mass Number (A) - Atomic Number (Z)
Example: Carbon (C)

Atomic Number Z = 6 (6 protons, 6 electrons)
Mass Number A = 12
Neutrons = 12 - 6 = 6 neutrons
Configuration: 2, 4 (2 electrons in K shell, 4 in L shell)

Electronic Configuration (Bohr Model)

Electrons fill the shells (orbits) around the nucleus in a specific order following rules:

Rules for Filling Electrons
  • Shells are named K, L, M, N (from innermost to outermost)
  • Maximum electrons in shell n = 2n
  • K shell (n=1): max 2 electrons | L shell (n=2): max 8 | M shell (n=3): max 18
  • The outermost shell cannot have more than 8 electrons
  • Electrons fill inner shells first before going to outer shells

Bohr Model Sodium Atom (Z=11)

Sodium (Na): Electronic configuration 2, 8, 1 1 electron in outermost shell makes it highly reactive!

Valence Electrons & Reactivity

Electrons in the outermost shell are called valence electrons. They determine chemical reactivity.

Sodium (Na) has 1 valence electron easily loses it forms Na reacts violently with water!
Neon (Ne) has 8 valence electrons complete shell very stable, doesn't react with anything (noble gas).
Chlorine has 7 valence electrons needs 1 more very reactive, grabs electrons from sodium ? NaCl (salt)!

Isotopes and Isobars

Isotopes

Atoms of the same element with the same atomic number (Z) but different mass numbers (A). They have different numbers of neutrons.

Isobars

Atoms of different elements with the same mass number (A) but different atomic numbers (Z).

? Example

Calcium (Ca): Z=20, A=40
Argon (Ar): Z=18, A=40

Both have A=40 but are completely different elements that makes them isobars!

Quick Quiz Chapter 4

1. Who discovered the electron?
J.J. Thomson discovered the electron in 1897 using cathode ray tube experiments. He proposed the Plum Pudding model of the atom.
2. An atom has atomic number 11 and mass number 23. How many neutrons does it have?
Neutrons = Mass Number - Atomic Number = 23 - 11 = 12. This is Sodium (Na) with configuration 2,8,1.
3. The maximum number of electrons in the L shell (n=2) is:
Using formula 2n: for L shell (n=2), max electrons = 2(2) = 8. K=2, L=8, M=18.
4. Isotopes of an element have the same:
Isotopes have the SAME atomic number (same element, same protons) but DIFFERENT mass numbers (different neutrons). E.g., C-12 and C-14 are both Carbon.
5. Rutherford's gold foil experiment led to the discovery of:
Rutherford's experiment proved that a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus exists at the centre of the atom. Most of the atom is empty space!
